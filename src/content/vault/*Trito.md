##  Introduction
*Trito stands as an enigmatic yet significant figure in Proto-Indo-European mythology, primarily associated with water and the sacred number three. His importance is reflected in various Indo-European traditions where his name and characteristics appear in divine figures associated with water, wisdom, and cosmic order.

---

## Etymology

- **Root meaning**: From Proto-Indo-European *tritos meaning "third"
- **Language of origin**: Proto-Indo-European
- **Variants and evolution**: 
  - Sanskrit: Trita Āptya
  - Avestan: Thrita
  - Greek: Triton
- **Symbolic implications**: The number three in his name suggests completeness and sacred order

---

##  Cultural Context

- **Civilization**: Proto-Indo-European
- **Geographic Region**: Pontic-Caspian steppe (hypothesized PIE homeland)
- **Historical Period**: c. 4500–2500 BCE
- **Alternative Names**:
  - Trita Āptya (Vedic)
  - Thrita (Iranian)
  - Triton (Greek)

## Myths and Legends

**Main Myth**  
In various Indo-European traditions, *Trito appears as a figure who battles against a three-headed water serpent ([[*Ngʷhi]]) or dragon, prefiguring later hero-dragon conflicts in various mythologies.

**Cultural Significance**  
- Associated with the sacred number three
- Connected to water and healing arts
- Prototype for later dragon-slaying heroes
